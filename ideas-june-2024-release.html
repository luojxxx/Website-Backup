CO2 sequestration and recycling

The global amount of CO2 produced per year is 37 gigatons and to put this into context 11 gigatons are transported by maritime shipping each year. So we need 3 times more than everything we ship overseas to handle the amount of CO2 we’re producing, this is the scale of the problem which means we need something of similar scale to tackle it.

My suggestion is taking agricultural waste, burning it, and either injecting the CO2 into the earth or recycle the CO2 into synthetic fuels. About 52% of the land in the US is used for agriculture, according to wikipedia 80% of 8.25 gigatons consists of global agricultural wastes per year from just the top four crops grown, and in the US agricultural waste is sold for around $55 per dry ton. Overall, it can be seen that the scale for carbon capture is there along with the economics with a $55 (or less in other countries) per ton compared to the $100 per ton of the proposed carbon tax. The CO2 can then be injected into basalt rocks underground to mineralize and be stored for thousands of years or turned into biofuels. The energy generated can be sold on the market or used in the process to generate the biofuels, the money generated could then be used to offset the cost of the operation. Overall, this may not be the silver bullet to defeating climate change, but it’ll be a good shot.

Update (7/17/24): It turns out there’s a company that emerged one month after I originally conceived of the design Arbor Energy. Also separate from Arbor energy as another related idea, agricultural waste could be ground up with water to create a slurry (maybe with preservatives) and injected into old gas and oil wells (or artificially created wells through methods like fracking). The off gases like CO2, methane, and etc would likely be contained in the well or they could be sold as a biofuel. The off gas CO2 and fossil fuels contribution to global airborne CO2 could be offset by pumping more slurry into the ground. Also not all the solids/liquids will turn into gas, this is just the same thing as naturally occurring fossil fuels and there’s definitely CO2 sequestered in this process. Finally agricultural waste is more dense in carbon than CO2 gas, because the carbons in agricultural wastes are in giant chains whereas CO2 gas is a single carbon connected to two heavy oxygen atoms. So 1 ton of agricultural waste technically sequesters more carbon than 1 ton of CO2 gas, so the impact of this process could be a very significant portion of the solution to climate change. Finally there’s the optics, people pumping oil/gas out of wells, while other people are putting it back in wells.

Update (10/23/2024): It turns out there’s a new company also pursuing a similar goal of burying CO2 Graphyte. Their plan is to take leftovers from agricultural and the timber industry to be turned into dried blocks that are individually packaged and then stored underground. I have to say I really like this idea, could be the bullet we’ve been looking for. Although the pumping plant slurry into old oil/gas wells could still work in parallel, plus the off-gas works as a biofuel.

Rescuing clinical trials through DNA sequencing

Basically some clinical trials may have failed due to it only working on a subset of the population, and many effective therapies are then thrown away. However, even with drugs that pass FDA certification many drugs sometimes works and sometimes doesn’t for a particular person. (This is particularly apparent with my own experiences with various psychiatric meds). Therefore we maybe able to rescue a drug from the trashcan by DNA sequencing to identify whether there’s a particular genotype where the drug does work. And it is already happening, ivacaftor for cystic fibrosis, trastuzumab for breast cancer, and etc. This rescues billions of dollars, advances personalized medicine, and provides relief for countless individuals.

P2P Home Sales

So what if the owner sold the house directly to the buyer with the seller acting as the bank. The seller gets the interest payments plus the sale of the house. This could allow the seller to offer a lower principal or lower rate than the bank and this could lead to lower housing costs. Imagine cutting out the bank and the real estate agents, there could be huge savings. It’s interesting how renting a home is p2p but buying one generally isn’t. I think a mediator could still play a role similar to how AirBnb mediates rentals, but with the money saved, the seller, buyer, and mediator could all still win.

Update (10/3/24): So I recently discovered that there are people working on my idea here. Basically my idea is to use a bracelet or something to modulate the electric field of the body, so that it can be picked up by something like a touchscreen to transmit information, therefore providing things like authentication. Overall, a very interesting idea.

Aging theories and experiments

Update (11/12/24): One of my aging theories has recently come to light, you can see it here. Basically there’s an evolutionary advantage to aging, by making individuals age, it makes room for their offspring who could be better adapted to their environment. Imagine a petri dish where it’s full and can no longer support new individuals, the new progeny don’t get created, die of an early death, or don’t grow fully due to lack of resources, and the older population remain evolutionarily stagnant. Sure, the habitat or changes in habitat can still cause a portion of them to die, providing room for replacement, but they would still be slower to evolve to those changes. This would also fit into the trend where animals in a safe niche tend to live longer, since there isn’t as much evolutionary pressure on them like elephants, naked mole rats, whales, etc. Overall, there isn’t another conceived evolutionary benefit to aging. Aging is otherwise detrimental to the organisms reproductive success, not only does it reduce the available time to reproduce, organisms that reproduce multiple times can not do so indefinitely. This theory can also work alongside other theories like the disposable soma theory where as long as the organism can reproduce what happens to the main body afterwards doesn’t matter; not only does it not matter, it’s good for evolution. Luckily this doesn’t have to be a mystery, there’s actually a very specific experiment that would provide some pretty convincing evidence either way and it answers another paradox I found (also the paradox provides evidence towards this theory of aging). However, that experiment will still be a mystery for now while the answers will be fascinating.

Update (12/5/2024): Since the release of Research: Aging Bioinformatics, I’ve released the critical question. Why is it that a mutation in just a single gene allowed C. elegans to double their lifespan, yet it did not already occur due to evolution? It’s such a huge win, so biologically advantageous, yet evolution has apparently so far missed it. My answer is what I’ve detailed above, in certain conditions it’s evolutionarily advantageous to age sooner. However, to put this theory to rest, I propose we run a specific experiment. In basically like an ecology study in a petri dish, test the long lived worms vs the short lived worms (or another model organism like flies/yeast with shorter reproduction cycles) in a head to head competition to see which one takes over the population. Or could be in separate petri dishes and see which ones have a larger population. And the idea is to apply evolutionary pressures like lack of resources, predation, modifying the pH or salinity of the petri dish, etc. If the short lived worms win out, it would provide some pretty compelling evidence that aging could be evolutionarily advantageous.
